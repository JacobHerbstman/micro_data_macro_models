\section{My Pond}

Broadly, the pond I hope to fish in is in urban, housing, and spatial economics. 
I am interested in the economics of agglomeration and why cities are such prominent drivers of wage and productivity growth, along with innovation. 
I also am very interested in the political economy of urban growth, and why people in high demand areas are so opposed to new housing, particularly multifamily housing. 
And finally, I want to complement the above questions by also researching the dynamics of cities and how new development and amenities affect inflows and outflows of different types of residents (i.e. ``gentrification'').

\section{Specific Questions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item What makes housing and transportation so much more expensive to build in the United States? 

    I think this question is essential to the future of infrastructure investment in the United States. 
    The United States desparately needs infrastructure expansion in housing, transit, energy, and other areas. 
    Despite investing historic amounts of money at times into these types of projects (such as CA high speed rail or the Biden IRA) we often build very little, 
    if anything, at all. When we do agree to build something, it takes ever-increasing amounts of time and money that seem nearly unlimited (Clark and Lake 3 year renovation timeline, 1 Billion/mile Red Line Extenstion, etc.). 
    There are a variety of explanations for what could be driving this lack of abiity to build. 
    Regulatory uncertainty and ``time to build'' \cite{budish_firms_2015}, procurement design and competition \cite{currier_competition_2026}, and differences in labor and paperwork requirements between the US and other countries is a good start, but we are just starting to get a handle on why the US is so stagnant in these areas.
    I think there is a lot more work to be done in this burgeoning field to truly diagnose and reform specific institutions that would allow us to build at the rate and cost of peer countries, or closer to it. 

    For example, Evan Mast at Notre Dame is doing ongoing research on the gap in construction costs between LIHTC and market rate housing, and trying to decompose those cost gaps into various channels. 
    Due to the web of funding required for LIHTC projects from federal, state, and local sources, developers on those projects have to navigate two or three layers of 
    regulatory requirements which add up to more than double the per sq. ft cost of those projects. And even for market rate housing, there is a huge gap in costs between states like California and Texas. 
    I think how we have gotten to this point is fascinating, and I am interested in how regulatory complexity, union requirements, and other factors play in to these differences. 

    The answers to these questions would be of interest to policymakers at every level, since all levels of government are struggling with cost and complexity bloat in their projects, deterring policymakers from even thinking big in the first place. 

    \item What are the net effects of gentrification, and what do those effects look like across the income distribution? What are the dynamics around neighborhood change more generally? 
    
    A lot of policymakers are wrestling with this right now. Housing researchers have largely come around to the view that we need relaxed zoning and faster permitting to bring down housing costs in expensive cities. But we know much less about who actually wins and loses when neighborhoods change, even when new supply is a net positive overall.
    Recent work is making progress here. \cite{couture_income_2024} show that as top incomes rise, high-income households sort into downtowns, which pushes up housing prices across the board, including in lower-quality neighborhoods where low-income renters live, and sets off a process where those neighborhoods get converted into high-quality ones. 
    Low-income downtown renters end up unambiguously worse off, even after you account for better public amenities and homeowner capital gains. 
    The model also suggests that policies aimed at containing gentrification downtown (like taxing high-end development) may just push the upgrading out to the suburbs without actually reducing welfare inequality.
    
    \cite{hong_redevelopment_2025} study a related case: a teardown tax near the 606 trail in Chicago. The policy was meant to stop developers from knocking down affordable multifamily buildings and replacing them with expensive single-family homes. They find it does help low-income renters by keeping older, cheaper housing stock intact, but it ends up hurting middle-income households the most. Restricting the supply of high-end units forces wealthier households to compete for middle-quality housing instead, bidding up rents in that segment. On top of that, the policy pushes redevelopment pressure into nearby untreated neighborhoods, raising rents there too.
    The distributional consequences of neighborhood change, and of the policies we design to manage it, are often counterintuitive. Gentrification can bring real benefits like better amenities and lower crime, but the costs fall unevenly across incomes and across space. I think there's a lot of room for more work on how to weigh these competing forces and figure out which policy designs can protect vulnerable residents without triggering the kind of unintended spillovers that Hong and Macek document. Local officials in housing-constrained metros understand they need more supply but also want to avoid displacement, and right now they don't have the evidence base to navigate that tradeoff well.

    \item How does the structure of local government decision-making affect who gets represented at the local level? Does this affect the distribution of housing supply, 
    local population growth, and municipal fiscal sustainability and health? 

    This one is a little less organized, but this question is broadly about who makes the decisions at the local level. 
    I currently am writing a paper on aldermanic prerogative in Chicago, and have been thinking a lot about how regulatory discretion creates uncertainty at 
    the local level and how fragmented decision-making structures can underprovide housing and density relative to the social planner, assuming people 
    have a distate for density \cite{gyourko_distaste_2024}. There has also been substantial local debate in Chicago about if 50 aldermen is too many, 
    because they don't represent diverse-enough swaths of the city to take the overall city's interests into account, and instead try to rent-seek on behalf of their small set of consituents. 
    I am interested if there is a way to study the ``optimal'' level at which we make municipal-level decisions, and how much that could change things. 
    \cite{mast_warding_2024} shows that moving to ward-based electoral systems reduce housing supply through exactly this mechansim. 
    In addition, if this suboptimal level of decision-making causes lower housing supply, it may affect aggregate population growth and municipal fiscal health as well too, 
    as seen with Chicago's recent budget crises and many policymakers saying the only way out of the budget crisis is to ``grow our way out''. I am curious if that statement holds any water empirically, and if I can study it. 
    There is also the issue of TIF (Tax Increment Financing Districts) which have been used to spur development at the cost of near-term property tax revenue for a city. 
    In Chicago, each aldermen handles the TIF districts within their ward individually, leading to lots of heterogeneity in how much the developers in that TIF are required to be on time and on budget. 
    This other angle of municipal-level decision making structures and discretion also has large implications for municipal financial health which I hope to explore. 

    There have been lots of talks about shrinking the size of the city council and reforming the TIF process in Chicago, so these questions would be directly applicable to policymakers at the state and local level here. 

\end{enumerate}

In a similar vein, it also encapsulates all of the regulatory uncertainty developers experience at the local level, the structure of local decision-making, and the political economy of density and urban form. As a result, 
I think this picture summarizes many of the major questions in the urban and spatial economics literature and ties them together, even if indirectly.

\section{Template Papers}

I have several papers I treat as templates for how to do good work in this space.
The one I keep coming back to most is \cite{rollet_zoning_2025}, which is my current north star for a job market paper.
I'm pretty confident it'll be in a top journal and heavily cited before long.
What I love about it is that it goes straight at a core debate in urban economics: is upzoning enough to actually increase housing supply?
He makes a convincing case that, in the short term, it probably isn't.
Beyond the question, he pairs careful, novel data work with a structural model that lets him run the important counterfactuals.
He assembled a new dataset covering every parcel in NYC from 2004 to 2022, which required years of manually digitizing zoning maps and building an algorithm to help.
I want to do something similar in my aldermanic prerogative project, potentially collecting paper records from meetings between developers and the city's planning and buildings departments.
He then feeds his empirical estimates of how sluggishly density responds to upzoning into a dynamic model with developers, which lets him pin down the mechanisms that make redevelopment expensive and hard to incentivize.

My second template is \cite{bordeu_commuting_2025}, also a recent job market paper, now R\&R at the Review of Economic Studies.
It has shaped how I think about fragmented decision making and the political economy of local governments more than almost anything else I've read.
She builds a model where municipalities in a metro area each invest in commuting infrastructure to maximize their own land value, showing theoretically that this produces systematic underinvestment near municipal borders (since infrastructure there benefits neighbors too) and potential overinvestment at core locations.
She then takes the model to Santiago, Chile, where she documents a sharp discontinuity in road density at municipal borders that lines up exactly with what the theory predicts.
Estimating the model structurally, she finds a centralized metropolitan planner would spend $44-60\%$ more on infrastructure and raise welfare $1.4-1.7\%$, with roughly $30\%$ of those gains coming from reallocation rather than extra spending.
That progression is exactly what I want to do for Chicago's ward system.
I think about aldermanic prerogative the same way she thinks about Santiago's municipalities: fragmented local governments making decisions that generate spillovers, leading to underprovision of a public good.
In her case it's roads; in mine it's housing permits.

The third is \cite{diamond_effects_2019}, a pure empirical paper with no structural model.
What makes it work is the identification: it comes directly from knowing the ins and outs of San Francisco's rent control laws.
In 1994, rent control was extended to small multifamily buildings built before 1980 but not to those built after, creating a sharp discontinuity that lets them cleanly compare tenants in controlled versus uncontrolled buildings.
They use Infutor address history data to track individual migration patterns over time, measuring who stays, who leaves, and where displaced tenants actually go.
They find rent control reduced displacement by about 15 percentage points and was especially effective at keeping older, lower-income tenants in the city.
But landlords responded by converting units to condos or redeveloping outright, shrinking the rental stock and raising rents for everyone else.
I want something like this in my toolkit alongside the model-based work of Rollet and Bordeu.
The Infutor data is something I want to explore for displacement patterns in Chicago, and the broader lesson for me is that if you know the institutional details well enough to find a clean natural experiment, you can answer important questions without a model.
That said, the paper also shows the limits of pure empirical work: they can measure partial equilibrium effects in San Francisco, but can't easily run the kind of citywide counterfactuals that Bordeu or Hong and Macek can with their models.
