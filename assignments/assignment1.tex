\section{My Pond}

Broadly, the pond I hope to fish in is in urban, housing, and spatial economics. 
I am interested in the economics of agglomeration and why cities are such prominent drivers of wage and productivity growth, along with innovation. 
I also am very interested in the political economy of urban growth, and why people in high demand areas are so opposed to new housing, particularly multifamily housing. 
And finally, I want to complement the above questions by also researching the dynamics of cities and how new development and amenities affect inflows and outflows of different types of residents (i.e. ``gentrification'').

\section{Specific Questions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item What makes housing and transportation so much more expensive to build in the United States? 

    I think this question is essential to the future of infrastructure investment in the United States. 
    Between regulatory uncertainty \cite{budish_firms_2015}, procurement design \cite{currier_competition_2026}, and potentially differences in labor and paperwork costs and requirements between the US and other countries, 
    The United States desparately needs infrastructure expansion in housing, transit, energy, and other areas. 
    Despite investing historic amounts of money at times into these types of projects (such as CA high speed rail or the Biden IRA) we often build very little, 
    if anything, at all. When we do agree to build something, it takes ever-increasing amounts of time and money that seem nearly unlimited (Clark and Lake 3 year renovation timeline, 1 Billion/mile Red Line Extenstion, etc.). 
    While the research cited above on regulatory uncertainty and procurement competition are good starts, I think there is a lot more work to be done in this burgeoning field to truly diagnose and reform specific institutions that would allow us to build at the rate and cost of peer countries, or closer to it. 

    For example, Evan Mast at Notre Dame is doing ongoing research on the gap in construction costs between LIHTC and market rate housing, and trying to decompose those cost gaps into various channels. 
    Due to the web of funding required for LIHTC projects from federal, state, and local sources, developers on those projects have to navigate two or three layers of 
    regulatory requirements which add up to more than double the per sq. ft cost of those projects. And even for market rate housing, there is a huge gap in costs between states like California and Texas. 
    I think how we have gotten to this point is fascinating, and I am interested in how regulatory complexity and union requirements play in to these differences. 

    The answers to this question would be of interest to policymakers at every level, since all levels of government are struggling with cost and complexity bloat in their projects, deterring policymakers from even thinking big in the first place. 

    \item What are the net effects of gentrification, and what do those effects look like across the income distribution? What are the dynamics around neighborhood change more generally? 
    
    This is another question that I think lots of policymakers are grappling with.
    I think broadly, housing policy people have converged toward agreement that more housing supply and relaxed zoning and permitting regulations will be needed to 
    bring down the skyrocketing housing costs in major urban metros in the US. However, much less is known about the potential winners and losers of this needed policy, even if it is on-net welfare improving. 
    I think there are a lot of interesting angles to this question and we are just starting to really understand how to think about this question. 
    \cite{couture_income_2024,hong_redevelopment_2025} both show how when gentrification occurs (more people in the top end of the income distribution come in), 
    it primarily squeezes the middle of the income distribution. If lots of housing supply, or income growth causing the reduction of housing supply (deconversions, recently heavily taxed in the 606 trail area in Chicago)
    cause substantial displacement, I think policymakers need to take that worry and reduction in quality of life seriously. On the other hand, ``gentrification'' also 
    often leads to more amenities and lower crime, and I am not sure we know exactly how we should balance those two very important and competing forces. 
    The answers to this question will inform many local officials in major metropolitan areas, which are all housing-constrained currently. They understand they need more housing supply, 
    but also often claim to want to avoid gentrification and displacement. More evidence on exactly how well-founded these fears are, and the relative magnitudes of the welfare changes between incumbents and new residents, is important 
    for guiding their decision making. 

    \item How does the structure of local government decision-making affect who gets represented at the local level? Does this affect the distribution of housing supply, 
    local population growth, and local budgets and municipal fiscal sustainability and health? 

    This one is a little less organized, but this question is broadly about who makes the decisions at the local level. 
    I currently am writing a paper on aldermanic prerogative in Chicago, and have been thinking a lot about how regulatory discretion creates uncertainty at 
    the local level and how fragmented decision-making structures can underprovide housing and density relative to the social planner, assuming people 
    have a distate for density \cite{gyourko_distaste_2024}. There has also been substantial local debate in Chicago about if 50 aldermen is too many, 
    because they don't represent diverse-enough swaths of the city to take the overall city's interests into account, and instead try to rent-seek on behalf of their small set of consituents. 
    I am interested if there is a way to study the ``optimal'' level at which we make municipal-level decisions, and how much that could change things. 
    \cite{mast_warding_2024} shows that moving to ward-based electoral systems reduce housing supply through exactly this mechansim. 
    In addition, if this suboptimal level of decision-making causes lower housing supply, it may affect aggregate population growth and municipal fiscal health as well too, 
    as seen with Chicago's recent budget crises and many policymakers saying the only way out of the budget crisis is to ``grow our way out''. I am curious if that statement holds any water empirically, and if I can study it. 
    There is also the issue of TIF (Tax Increment Financing Districts) which have been used to spur development at the cost of near-term property tax revenue for a city. 
    In Chicago, each aldermen handles the TIF districts within their ward individually, leading to lots of heterogeneity in how much the developers in that TIF are required to be on time and on budget. 
    This other angle of municipal-level decision making structures and discretion also has large implications for municipal financial health which I hope to explore. 

    There have been lots of talks about shrinking the size of the city council and reforming the TIF process in Chicago, so these questions would be directly applicable to policymakers at the state and local level here. 

\end{enumerate}

While most industries in the United States produce more output per worker than they did in the 1970s, construction has seen a flattening or even slight decline. I like showing this trend because it encapsulates
all of the questions I am interested in. The hypothesis in \cite{damico_why_2024} is that this trend is due to land-use regulation, which has limited the economies of scale from construction firms and left them smaller than the counterfactual, 
unable to profit from economies of scale and reducing the incentive to innovate. 
In a similar vein, it also encapsulates all of the regulatory uncertainty developers experience at the local level, the structure of local decision-making, and the political economy of density and urban form. As a result, 
I think this picture summarizes many of the major questions in the urban and spatial economics literature and ties them together, even if indirectly.

\section{Template Papers}

I have many papers I look to as templates for how to do good work in this space. The first is relatively recent but is my current ``north star'' for a job market paper, which is \cite{rollet_zoning_2025}. 
I am quite confident this paper will in the very near future be in a top journal and heavily cited, so I am including it. 
The first reason I love this paper is because it cuts at the heart of a core urban economics debate: is upzoning enough to increase housing supply? 
And he answers, incredibly convincingly, that it is likely not in the short term. 
The other reasons I love this paper are that he combines very careful, novel data work with a cool model that helps him ask the important counterfactuals. 
He collects a whole new dataset on every parcel in NYC from 2004-2022, which took ostensibly years of manual digitization of zoning maps and he even wrote an algorithm to assist him. 
I want to do similar work in my aldermanic privilege project, collecting potentially paper records of meetings between developers and the department of planning and development and/or department of buildings.
He then uses his empirical results on the sluggish response of density to upzoning changes in a dynamic model with developers to show the exact mechanisms that cause redevelopment to be expensive and tricky to incentivize. 

My second paper is also a recent job market paper, and is R\&R at the Review of Economic Studies \cite{bordeu_commuting_2025}.
This paper has influenced heavily how I think about fragmented decision making and the political economy of local governments. 
The paper shows first how in a spatial model with local infrastructure provision that municipalities underinvest in infrastructure near their borders, since some of that benefit spills over to neighbors but the municipality bears the full cost. 
She does a great job bringing Chilean data together with a really powerful model to show how counterfactuals where decisions are made at a more aggregated level lead to welfare-improving outcomes, which is how I am currently thinking about the ward system and housing supply in Chicago, in line with \cite{mast_warding_2024}.

The third paper is \cite{diamond_effects_2019}. This paper handles one of the most contentious topics in urban economics, which is rent control and its effects on both tenants and housing supply. 
The identification strategy is incredibly smart and based on deep understandings of the instituional details of San Francisco's rent control laws. 
They also use the Infutor Data I want to explore to look at who lives in rent controlled units, and analyze in and out migration patterns. They find that it allows people to stay in the city who otherwise would have been pushed out, 
but also reduces housing supply which overall probably led to even more displacement. This paper informs how I think about rent control and approaches to housing shortages more generally, 
and is a perfect incentive of simple but effective economics thinking, which is that incentives matter and we should take them seriously.
